# Sell the Way You Buy

Before sending an email, leaving a voicemail, or delivering a pitch — ask: "Would I respond to this if I received it?" If the honest answer is no, rewrite it. This is the foundational test for everything in this skill.

## Why Buyer Psychology Matters for Sprites

Oliver's prospects are smart, busy marketing operators who get pitched constantly. They can smell a template from the first line. The way to stand out isn't louder — it's more human. Understand how they think, what triggers their defenses, and what makes them lean in.

## Three Principles of Buyer Behavior

### 1. People Buy Emotionally, Then Justify Logically
The decision to buy happens in the emotional brain first. Logic comes second to rationalize.

**What this means in practice:**
- Lead with the problem, not the solution. When they FEEL the pain, they'll want the fix.
- Stories beat specs. "H.M. Cole was drowning in agency retainers" lands harder than "Sprites has API integrations."
- Social proof triggers belonging. "Teams like yours are doing X" is more powerful than "our product does X."

### 2. The Status Quo Bias
People prefer the current state even when a better option exists. The pain of change feels bigger than the pain of staying.

**What this means:** Don't attack their current setup — they'll defend it. Instead, make the future state feel safe and easy. Anchor to what they already believe: "You already know your agency isn't moving fast enough — the question is what replaces them."

### 3. Reactance: Why Pushing Harder Backfires
When people feel pressured, they resist — even if they agree with you. Hard closes create ghosting. "Limited time offers" feel manipulative. "You need to act now" triggers defiance.

**Instead:** Give the buyer control. "This might not be a fit, and that's totally fine. But here's what we've seen work for teams like yours."

## Discovery Framework

Discovery is the most important skill in sales. The person asking questions controls the conversation AND builds trust. See [references/discovery-questions.md](references/discovery-questions.md) for the full question library.

### The WHAT-WHY-IMPACT Model

Go three levels deep on every pain point:

1. **WHAT:** "What does your current process look like for managing ad campaigns?"
2. **WHY:** "What's driving that approach?" or "Why do you think it's set up that way?"
3. **IMPACT:** "What does that cost you in terms of time, money, or results?"

The third level is where deals are made. When the prospect quantifies their own pain, they sell themselves.

### Discovery Rules
- Don't ask questions you could answer with 5 minutes of research
- Don't ask more than 2 questions in a row without adding value (insight, reaction, or empathy)
- React to what they say — "That's a big deal" / "I hear that a lot" — don't just take notes silently
- Weave questions into conversation, don't save them for one block

## Positioning

### Lead with the Problem, Not the Product

**Wrong:** "Sprites is an AI marketing platform that connects to Meta and Google Ads..."
**Right:** "Most marketing teams I talk to are using ChatGPT or Gemini for insights — but still executing everything manually. The gap between knowing what to do and doing it is where results die."

The problem statement should make them nod before Sprites is ever mentioned.

### The Contrast Frame
"Right now, your team is spending [X hours/week] inside ad platforms making manual adjustments. That means you're paying salary for someone to do what AI handles in real-time. Imagine if that person was freed up for strategy and creative instead."

### Anchor to Existing Beliefs
Don't introduce new ideas — connect to ones they already hold:
- "You're already using AI for copywriting — Sprites just closes the execution loop."
- "You already know your agency isn't fast enough — the question is what replaces them."

## Demo Structure

See [references/demo-playbook.md](references/demo-playbook.md) for the full demo structure and common mistakes.

**The core principle:** Every feature shown must connect to a problem THEY told you about. Features without context are noise.

1. **Recap pain (30 sec):** "You mentioned X, Y, Z are the biggest challenges. Let me show you how we handle each one."
2. **Show, don't tell:** For each pain point, show the feature that solves it. Pause: "Does that address what you were describing?"
3. **Case study bridge:** "This is exactly what H.M. Cole was dealing with. Here's what happened."
4. **Ask for reaction:** "How does this compare to what you were expecting?"
5. **Next step:** "The natural next step is to connect one account and see what Sprites surfaces. Takes 10 minutes. Want to do that now?"

## How to Apply This Skill

When Oliver needs help with calls or demos:
1. Ask what he knows about the prospect's specific pain (not generic ICP pain — THEIR pain)
2. Help identify the right discovery questions for this person's role and situation
3. Build talk tracks that lead with their world, not Sprites features
4. If a demo went poorly, review what happened — usually the issue is pitching before discovering
5. Apply the "would I respond to this?" test to everything
