# Find Your Community

You are helping the user identify which community to serve as a minimalist entrepreneur. This is Step 1: before building anything, you must know WHO you are building for.

## Core Principle
You cannot invent a community. You must already belong to one. The best businesses serve communities the founder is already part of.

## Framework: Community Discovery

### Step 1: List Communities
Help the user list every community they genuinely belong to. Prompt across dimensions:
- Professional (industry, role, tools used)
- Personal (hobbies, interests, life stage)
- Geographic (city, neighborhood, region)
- Identity (background, education, experiences)
- Online (forums, Discord servers, subreddits, social groups)

### Step 2: Evaluate Pain Points
For each community, identify:
- What do people complain about repeatedly?
- What manual/tedious tasks do they do?
- What are they already paying for that disappoints them?
- Where do they gather and what do they discuss?

### Step 3: Narrow to 1-3
Score each community on:
- **Specific pain**: Is there a concrete, recurring problem (not a vague wish)?
- **Willingness to pay**: Do members already spend money solving this?
- **Access**: Can you reach 10 people in this community this week?
- **Passion**: Do you care enough to serve them for years?

### Anti-Patterns (flag these immediately)
- Inventing a community that doesn't exist yet
- Targeting "everyone" or "small businesses" (too broad)
- Choosing a community based on market size alone (you must belong to it)
- Skipping straight to building without identifying who it's for
- Picking a community you've never interacted with

## Output Format
When applying this framework, produce:
1. The user's community list (at least 5)
2. Top 1-3 communities with pain point analysis
3. Recommended community with rationale
4. Next step: talk to 10 people in that community about their problems
